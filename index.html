Bordieu's _Language and Symbolic Power_ (1993)

  • Utterances are not simply methods of communication to transport ideas

  • Medium of power that bears the social structures and histories at work

  • In politics, language is a means to express and operationalize these structures, revealing what is at stake.

How does this relate to the digital?

Arbitrary Distinction Between Programming Languages & Human Languages

  • Florian Cramer (Software Studies, "Language"): “[t]here is nothing 'natural' about spoken language; it is a cultural construct and thus just as 'artificial' as any formal machine controls languages. To call programming languages 'machine languages' doesn't solve the problem either, as it obscures that 'machine languages' are human creations” (qtd. in Brock, "One Hundred Thousand").

  • Noah Wardrip-Fruin (Expressive Processing): “the processes of digital media operate both on and in terms of humanly meaningful elements and structures” (qtd. in Brock).

  • Mark C. Marino (“Critical Code Studies”) on issues of separating symbolic action and machine function: it “neglects the meaning that code bears for its human audiences” (qtd. in Brock).

The Interrupt as per Dijkstra

  • "While the computer calculates at full speed, a piece of dedicated hardware monitors the outside world for completion signals from communication devices. When a completion is detected, the program under execution is interrupted after the current instruction and in such a fashion that it can be resumed at a later moment as if nothing had happened, thus instantaneously freeing the central processor for a suddenly more urgent task. After the interrupt the processor would execute a standard program establishing the source of the interruption and taking appropriate action." (Dijkstra, "My Recollections" 13)

Identifiers, Interrupt Vectors, & Handlers

"Within an operating system, the various kinds of interrupt signals are differentiated by an identifier, which is mapped to a short handler program by an 'interrupt vector.'" (Yuill 162)

_The New Rhetoric_ :: Ends/Means Relations in Argumentation

  • Ends should be framed by the rhetor as a positive causal relation to value, serving as a desired “ends” to a person's means.

  • Consequences, serving as the opposite to an end, seek to diminish the effects of this causal relation

  • The two can be used to distinguish or establish an object of argumentation
  • when an end becomes a mean, it typically has a depreciative effect, because it often involves an argument about what is the most valued end to achieve in a procedural system
